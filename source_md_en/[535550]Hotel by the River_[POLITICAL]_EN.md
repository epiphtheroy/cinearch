### Introduction: The Revelation

Interpretations vary.

One might interpret the poet Young-hwan's summoning of his estranged sons in *Hotel by the River* as a simple critique of Confucian patriarchal hierarchies in post-1997 Asian Financial Crisis South Korea. Or perhaps view his passive acceptance of isolation solely through the lens of feminist intersectionality amid the 2018 #MeToo movement in Seoul.

However, the action's true ethical-political significance emerges elsewhere.

The chosen element of **The Narrative Action** is Young-hwan's contemplative detachment from familial bonds, embodying a self-absorbed retreat that perpetuates emotional neglect under the guise of artistic introspection. The corresponding **Critical Lens** is Wendy Brown's Neoliberal Rationality, applied to South Korea's post-IMF economic reforms and the 2020s gig economy precarity.

This thesis argues that Young-hwan's action reveals how neoliberal ideology transforms personal ethics into market-driven individualism, mirroring South Korea's shift from collective welfare to entrepreneurial selfhood, thus subverting communal solidarity in a era of rising inequality.
