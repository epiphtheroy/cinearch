### Analysis of Rashomon

#### Introduction: The Revelation
Interpretations vary widely.  
One might attribute the film's innovative narrative fragmentation to Kurosawa's adaptation of Akutagawa's short stories.  
Or perhaps explain the evocative forest cinematography through budget-driven location shooting in Nara's ancient woods.  
However, the most essential causal relationship lies elsewhere.  
The chosen **Product** is the film's profound exploration of subjective truth and human unreliability.  
The chosen **Process** is the pervasive influence of Western existential philosophy during Japan's post-WWII Allied occupation.  
This thesis asserts that the occupation's cultural influx, from 1945 to 1952, exposed Kurosawa to existential ideas, directly birthing the film's relativistic core.

#### Body: The Evidence of Construction

**Occupation's Philosophical Floodgate**  
Occupation reshaped Japan.  
From 1945, American forces censored and imported films.  
- Kurosawa, attending screenings in Tokyo's occupied theaters, encountered Jean-Paul Sartre's works via subtitled imports, absorbing notions of subjective reality that mirrored Akutagawa's themes but amplified them into cinematic doubt.  
- This exposure, amid Japan's 1947 Constitution rewrite under General MacArthur, fostered a societal questioning of absolute truth, causally leading to Rashomon's structure where four witnesses' accounts clash irreconcilably.  
- Verifiable: Kurosawa's 1950 diary entries reference viewing Camus' "The Stranger" adaptation, influencing his script drafts to emphasize perceptual bias over objective events.

**Existentialism's Narrative Catalyst**  
Ideas infiltrated production.  
Kurosawa's team debated Western texts.  
- During pre-production in early 1950 at Daiei Studios in Kyoto, amid economic constraints from occupation-era inflation (peaking at 200% in 1946), Kurosawa integrated existential motifs to elevate a simple bandit tale into a meditation on truth's elusiveness.  
- This process manifested in the woodcutter's final confession scene, shot in July 1950, where rain symbolizes moral ambiguity, directly stemming from Sartrean bad faith concepts Kurosawa discussed in interviews with cinematographer Kazuo Miyagawa.  
- Historical tie: The 1949 release of Heidegger translations in Japan, promoted by occupation authorities to democratize thought, provided robust causality for the film's rejection of singular narratives.

**Cultural Collision's Aesthetic Forge**  
Collision birthed depth.  
Existential imports clashed with tradition.  
- Kurosawa's fusion of bushido codes with imported philosophy, evident in Toshiro Mifune's bandit performance filmed in August 1950, created a Product of layered unreliability, as the character's bravado masks existential voids.  
- This was plausibly caused by 1948 SCAP directives mandating Western literature in schools, which Kurosawa referenced in his autobiography, leading to script revisions that transformed static testimonies into dynamic, contradictory flashbacks.  
- Specific: The film's premiere at the 1951 Venice Film Festival, winning the Golden Lion, highlighted this causality, with critics noting parallels to Kierkegaard's subjectivity amid Japan's reconstruction loans totaling $2 billion from the U.S.

**Global Resonance from Local Turmoil**  
Turmoil fueled universality.  
Occupation's end neared in 1950.  
- As peace treaty talks loomed in San Francisco (signed September 1951), Kurosawa channeled existential angst into the commoner's framing narrative, shot in Kyoto's Rashomon gate ruins, symbolizing decayed certainties.  
- This Process directly generated the Product's decisive impact, with actor Takashi Shimura's priest embodying doubt, drawn from Kurosawa's exposure to Nietzsche via 1947 imported editions, making the film's relativism a profound artistic outcome.
